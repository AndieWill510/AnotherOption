# Option 1.1 — The Saxophone

Author: Kevin "Andie" Williams  
Status: Doorway pilot draft  
Project: You Have Another Option  
Source: FrontMatter/001-prelude-the-saxophone.md  
Safety: low direct crisis content; childhood abandonment / shame content present

---

## Doorway

Before we talk about Autism, before we talk about lifespan, before we talk about survival, I want to start with a saxophone.

I was eleven years old, standing in Texas winter cold before dawn, waiting for school and band and another day of trying to make something beautiful happen with an instrument that would not cooperate.

Music was one of the few beautiful things available to me. I practiced. I tried. I stared at the sheet music so hard it felt like I could burn a hole through the page.

Every time, I was fourth chair.

Out of four.

That number mattered. Not because fourth chair is a moral failure. It is not. It mattered because children know when the room has decided who belongs. They know when the glances mean, "Oh. Him again." They know when trying harder does not make people kinder.

So I tried harder.

I practiced more.

I tried to make the notes.

The notes would not come.

## Pressure

When you are a child, you do not usually get to inspect the instrument.

You inspect yourself.

You assume the problem is your mouth, your hands, your focus, your discipline, your worth. You assume everyone else hears something true about you in the sound you cannot make.

That is a terrible thing to teach a child.

It is also one of the ordinary ways Autistic people are trained to misunderstand their own lives.

We are handed tools for social connection that were not built for our bodies, our timing, our sensory systems, our literalness, our intensity, our honesty, or our ways of loving. Then, when the tools do not work, we are told the failure lives inside us.

So we practice harder.

We mask harder.

We study people harder.

We stare at the social sheet music and try not to lose our place.

And still the room says: fourth chair.

## Reframe

My tutor eventually asked to see the saxophone.

He played it.

To both our surprise, he sounded like me.

Then he took apart the mouthpiece and found the crack.

The problem was not my effort.

The problem was not my desire.

The problem was not that I did not care enough about music.

The mouthpiece was cracked.

I was never going to make those notes.

That sentence did not give me back the lost semester. It did not give me back the cold mornings, the social shame, the crush who did not return my warmth, or the feeling of being seen as less than I was.

But it gave me something else.

It gave me the first mercy:

Maybe the failure was not me.

## Move

Try this gently.

Think of one place in your life where you have spent years trying harder and harder, but the notes still will not come.

Do not decide yet whether to quit.

Do not decide yet whether to forgive anyone.

Just ask:

> What if the mouthpiece is cracked?

What if the tool is wrong?

What if the environment is wrong?

What if the expectation is wrong?

What if your effort has been real this whole time?

That question may not solve the problem today.

But it can loosen the shame.

And loosened shame is sometimes the first breath of a life becoming possible again.

## Mercy

You may have been trying to make music through a broken mouthpiece for a very long time.

That does not mean you were broken.

It means we need to look at the instrument.
