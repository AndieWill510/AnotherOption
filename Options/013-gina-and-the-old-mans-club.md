# Option 1.13 — Gina and the Old Man's Club

Author: Kevin "Andie" Williams  
Status: Community section memoir draft  
Project: You Have Another Option  
Source: Sections/02-community-as-survival-infrastructure.md; Andie live dictation  
Safety: memoir / belonging / chosen-community arc; disclosure review required

---

## Doorway

Then walked in Gina.

Larger than her own life, much less everyone else's.

She was Beanie in the 21st century.

A flapper with a very punk rock edge.

She gave no shits about anyone's attitude.

And frankly, she was exactly what I needed.

We are friends still.

Some people enter a room like permission.

Not polite permission.

Not institutional permission.

Not the kind of permission that asks you to shrink first.

Gina entered like the room had already lost the argument.

## Pressure

When you have spent a life trying to earn your way into rooms, a person who refuses the room's judgment can feel almost impossible.

Not safe in the soft way.

Safe in the disruptive way.

Safe because she did not seem to organize herself around whether other people approved.

Safe because she carried her own weather.

Safe because some people show you that belonging does not always arrive quietly.

Sometimes it kicks the door open, lights a cigarette in the rules, and says:

Come on.

We're going somewhere.

## Reframe

I spent a magical week in the Caribbean with Gina and met her own version of what she was about to introduce me to next.

Then came the question.

Did I want to come to an art opening?

Yes.

It was Peter's.

Oh hell yes.

That decision, and meeting Lee, who owned the jewelry store where Peter's collection of portraits of the old man's club was showing, would prove fateful in the most delicious way.

Lee invited me to join the old man's club.

First for a single meeting.

Then as a member.

Lee did not invite anyone to join.

That is the thing about community as infrastructure: sometimes the beam that carries the house is an invitation someone almost never gives.

## Move

Think of one invitation that changed more than it seemed to change.

One door.

One yes.

Do not make it smaller because you did not know yet what it would become.

## Mercy

Belonging does not always arrive as a plan.

Sometimes it arrives as Gina.

Sometimes it arrives as an art opening.

Sometimes it arrives through a door nobody was supposed to open for you.
