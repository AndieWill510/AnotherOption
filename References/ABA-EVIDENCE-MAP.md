## Purpose

This file maps high-stakes ABA-related claims to the sources currently listed in `Chapters/REFERENCES.md`, separating what is provable from what is merely adjacent, contested, advocacy-based, journalistic, or missing. It covers historical origins, conversion-therapy overlap, PTSD/trauma claims, compliance and passing critiques, practitioner credential claims, and aversive-conditioning claims because these are credibility-critical: the book can speak with teeth, but not with slippage.

## Claim A: ABA shares historical roots with Lovaas-era conversion therapy

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
ABA and conversion therapy for gay or gender-nonconforming children share a historical lineage through Ivar Lovaas and behaviorist aversion/reinforcement methods.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 126: Lovaas interview hosted at DocPlayer — content unconfirmed
- Ref 128: LA Times obituary for Ivar Lovaas — journalism
- Ref 129: Crime and Justice PDF — content unconfirmed
- Ref 210: Rekers & Lovaas (1974), JABA DOI 10.1901/jaba.1974.7-173 — primary-source candidate added from earlier ABA review

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE for historical connection / CONTESTED for equivalence claim

**What these sources prove:**
The current source list supports a historically bounded claim that Lovaas is connected to both ABA’s early autism intervention history and behaviorist interventions aimed at suppressing gender-nonconforming behavior. Ref 210 appears to be the primary Rekers & Lovaas source and, once read, should carry the historical claim more cleanly than summaries or obituaries. Ref 128 can support public-history framing but remains journalism.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not prove that all modern ABA is conversion therapy, that modern ABA practitioners share Lovaas’s intent, or that every contemporary ABA program uses aversives. They also do not prove that harm mechanisms are identical between autistic children and LGBTQ+ conversion-therapy survivors. The book may argue continuity of behavioral logic, but not collapse the categories without jurisdiction- and practice-specific proof.

**Book treatment:**
Use precise lineage language: Lovaas-era behaviorism, aversion/reinforcement, suppression of disfavored behavior, and the historical overlap between autism intervention and gender/sexuality normalization. Do not write “ABA is conversion therapy everywhere.” Write that some critics and legal scholars argue that behavior-modification programs suppressing autistic identity expression belong in the same conversion-practice family, and then point to legal sources in Claim B.

**Action required:**
Read ref 210 before quoting. Read ref 126 before using any Lovaas wording. Verify ref 129 or remove it from ABA historical claims.

## Claim B: ABA or ABA-style intervention is legally classified with conversion practices in some jurisdictions or legal scholarship

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Some legal frameworks or legal scholars classify ABA-style suppression of autistic identity expression alongside conversion therapy or conversion practices.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 37: Born Perfect conversion-therapy ban tracker — advocacy tracker
- Ref 38: Victorian Change or Suppression Practices legislation — primary legal source
- Ref 39: Equality Caucus international ban list — advocacy / policy tracker
- Ref 40: Human Rights Campaign reparative therapy resource — advocacy resource
- Ref 140: Cambridge Canadian Journal of Law and Society article on autistic and trans children in Ontario — peer-reviewed legal scholarship

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE with jurisdiction precision / THIN if based only on advocacy trackers

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 38 can prove the exact language of the Victorian legal framework once the current enacted text is read and quoted carefully. Ref 140 can support the existence of a legal-scholarship argument linking autistic and trans children, clinical personhood, and conversion-practice reasoning. Refs 37, 39, and 40 help locate policy context but should not carry a statutory interpretation alone.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not prove that ABA is universally illegal, that any US federal law classifies ABA as conversion therapy, or that individual ABA practitioners have been prosecuted under conversion-therapy bans. They do not prove that all behavior intervention is conversion practice; the legally relevant question is usually suppression of identity expression, coercion, and jurisdiction-specific definitions.

**Book treatment:**
Use jurisdiction-specific wording: “In some legal and policy contexts, behavior-change practices aimed at suppressing identity expression are being analyzed alongside conversion practices.” Cross-reference `References/CONVERSION-THERAPY-LEGAL-EVIDENCE-MAP.md` for the legal-only map and avoid duplicating statutory analysis here.

**Action required:**
Read ref 38 for exact current statutory language. Read ref 140 for the actual argument and scope. Verify whether any US state law explicitly names autism, ABA, disability, or behavior intervention before making US-specific claims.

## Claim C: ABA exposure is associated with PTSD or trauma symptoms in autistic people

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
ABA exposure is associated with PTSD symptoms or trauma-like harm among autistic people.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 55: Embrace Autism PTSD article — advocacy / educational source, not peer-reviewed
- Ref 137: Emerald / Advances in Autism DOI 10.1108/AIA-08-2017-0016 — appears to be Kupferstein (2018), content inferred from DOI; verify before citing
- Refs 142–146: Autistic advocacy and lived-experience sources on ABA harm
- Ref 147: PMC7368197 — wrong source for ABA/PTSD

**Status:** CONTESTED for peer-reviewed association if ref 137 is confirmed / THIN for advocacy testimony / WRONG SOURCE for ref 147

**What these sources prove:**
If ref 137 is confirmed as Kupferstein (2018), it supports the limited claim that one peer-reviewed retrospective self-report study reported an association between ABA exposure and elevated PTSD symptom scores among autistic respondents. Refs 142–146 support the existence of consistent autistic survivor testimony and community critique. Survivor testimony is evidence of reported experience and should be treated seriously, clearly labeled, and not dismissed because it is not a randomized trial.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not prove ABA causes PTSD. A single retrospective, self-selected, self-report study cannot establish causality, prevalence, or generalizability. Advocacy sources do not prove clinical mechanism. Testimony does not prove that every ABA program harms every child, nor that all reported trauma arises from the same intervention components.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “At least one peer-reviewed study has reported an association between ABA exposure and elevated PTSD symptoms, but the study is methodologically contested and should not be treated as causal proof. Autistic survivor testimony is also extensive and must be named as lived-experience evidence.” Do not write: “Research proves ABA causes PTSD.”

**Action required:**
Read ref 137 and confirm title, sample, measures, recruitment, and limitations. Remove or reassign ref 147. Search for replications, systematic reviews, critiques, and responses before strengthening the claim.

## Claim D: ABA often prioritizes compliance, indistinguishability, or passing over autistic wellbeing

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
ABA and related autism outcome measures often reward appearing less autistic rather than improving self-determined wellbeing.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 135: Fortune reporting on ABA industry — journalism
- Ref 136: Forbes reporting on ABA business practices — journalism
- Ref 138: Frontiers in Psychiatry article on autistic client autonomy — peer-reviewed perspective
- Ref 141: Springer article — content unconfirmed
- Refs 142–146: advocacy / lived-experience sources
- Ref 209: AMA Journal of Ethics article on behavior-defined disability and passing — peer-reviewed ethics article

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE with source labels

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 209 directly supports the ethical critique that when disability is defined behaviorally, outcome measures can wrongly promote “passing” or typical appearance over person-centered goals. Ref 138 supports a broader autonomy critique in mental health treatment involving autistic clients, though it is a perspective article rather than an empirical trial. Refs 135–136 document industry and financial-incentive concerns as journalism. Refs 142–146 document autistic advocacy and lived-experience objections.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not prove every ABA program prioritizes compliance over wellbeing, or that compliance-focused outcomes are unique to ABA. They do not prove the size of the effect across the whole ABA industry. They also do not prove that reducing intrinsically harmful behavior is unethical; the critique applies most strongly when harmless autistic traits are targeted for suppression because they are stigmatized.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “A serious ethical critique of ABA and autism outcome measurement is that some interventions define success as appearing less autistic. That can conflict with self-determination when the targeted trait is harmless and only socially stigmatized.” Keep the distinction between reducing danger/distress and enforcing normalcy.

**Action required:**
Read ref 141 before use. Identify additional empirical work on autism intervention outcome measures, especially work distinguishing quality of life, autonomy, communication, safety, and compliance proxies.

## Claim E: ABA front-line delivery can involve low formal training relative to intervention intensity

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Registered Behavior Technicians can deliver intensive behavior-analytic services to autistic children after relatively limited training, under supervision.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 148: Discovery ABA salary source — industry source
- Ref 149: NCES education statistics — federal education data
- Ref 150: Psych Central professional statistics — health journalism
- Ref 151: The Tree Top ABA demographics — industry-adjacent
- Ref 152: Songbird Care RBT certification — industry source
- Ref 153: Elemy degree requirements — industry source
- Ref 154: EdSource teacher credential comparison — education journalism
- Ref 213: BACB Registered Behavior Technician page — credentialing authority

**Status:** PROVABLE for credential structure once BACB handbook is read / THIN for harm inference

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 213 is the appropriate primary source for the RBT credential structure and supervision relationship. It supports the claim that RBT is a paraprofessional certification and that RBTs practice under close supervision. Industry and journalism sources can help with labor-market context, but the credential claim should use BACB directly.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not prove that lower formal training causes harm, that all RBTs are unprepared, or that supervision is categorically inadequate. They also do not prove that teacher credential comparisons are perfectly equivalent; they are useful as reader-facing context, not as a controlled professional-risk analysis.

**Book treatment:**
Frame as a governance and risk question: “The front-line role is paraprofessional and supervised. Given the intensity and developmental stakes of the work, the training/supervision model deserves scrutiny.” Do not write that low training itself proves abuse.

**Action required:**
Read the current BACB RBT Handbook and capture exact eligibility, training, competency assessment, exam, supervision, and 2026-transition requirements. Use the handbook rather than ABA provider marketing pages.

## Claim F: ABA’s origins include aversive conditioning, including electric shock, and aversive devices persisted into recent regulatory history

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Early ABA used aversive interventions including electric shock, and electrical stimulation devices used as aversives remained a live regulatory issue into the 2020s.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 126: Lovaas interview — primary-source candidate, content unconfirmed
- Ref 128: LA Times obituary — journalism
- Ref 211: Federal Register 2020 FDA final rule banning electrical stimulation devices for self-injurious or aggressive behavior
- Ref 212: Reuters 2024 reporting on FDA proposal to ban certain shock devices again

**Status:** PROVABLE for FDA regulatory action / SUPPORTABLE for Lovaas-era history after source verification

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 211 proves that the FDA finalized a 2020 ban on electrical stimulation devices for self-injurious or aggressive behavior, finding that these devices posed an unreasonable and substantial risk that could not be corrected or eliminated by labeling. Ref 212 can support the later regulatory/reporting context, but it is journalism. Ref 126 and ref 128 can support historical claims about Lovaas-era aversives once read carefully.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not prove that current mainstream ABA uses electric shock. They do not prove that every aversive device use is identical to every ABA program. They do not prove that the FDA rule was a ban on ABA as a whole. The current claim must distinguish early ABA history, the Judge Rotenberg Center/device context, and contemporary mainstream ABA practice.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “ABA’s early history includes aversive conditioning, including electric shock in Lovaas-era work. Separately, US regulators treated electrical stimulation devices for self-injurious or aggressive behavior as dangerous enough to ban in 2020. That is not the same as saying all modern ABA uses shock.”

**Action required:**
Read ref 126 before quoting Lovaas. Confirm the procedural status of the FDA ban and later court/regulatory developments before publishing a final chronology.
