## Purpose

This file maps high-stakes ABA-related claims to the sources currently listed in `References/REFERENCES.md`, separating what is provable from what is merely adjacent, contested, advocacy-based, journalistic, or missing.

It covers:

```text
historical origins
conversion-therapy overlap
PTSD / trauma claims
compliance, passing, and outcome-measure critiques
practitioner credential / training claims
aversive-conditioning and electric-shock claims
industry and incentive claims
```

For each claim it records:

- which sources in the current list touch it;
- what those sources actually prove;
- what they do not prove;
- how the claim should be worded in reader-facing prose;
- what is missing and must be found before the claim can be made.

The working rule is not what can be cited.

It is what can be proved.

A source that touches a topic is not a source that proves a claim.

A source that proves a claim must be read, its actual content confirmed, and its limitations named.

This map is not a proof store.

It is a gap register, claim-control map, and scrutiny record.

It does not become a proof store until each entry has been verified and signed off.

## Drafting Gate

Before drafting or revising ABA / institutional-harm chapters or Options, check this file.

Applies especially to:

```text
Chapters/070.md
Chapters/080.md

```

Required workflow:

```text
References/REFERENCES.md -> References/ABA-EVIDENCE-MAP.md -> claim review -> reader-facing chapter/Option
```

Do not move directly from a raw URL in `REFERENCES.md` to reader-facing prose.

Do not use this map to make a claim stronger than the evidence register allows.

This file should not duplicate every reference in `References/REFERENCES.md`.

It should collect and evaluate the references that matter for ABA, institutional harm, compliance, conversion-history, intervention goals, outcome measures, workforce/training, and harm claims.

If a new ABA-relevant source is found:

1. add it to `References/REFERENCES.md`;
2. add or update the relevant claim entry here;
3. mark what the source proves, does not prove, and whether it has been verified;
4. only then allow reader-facing prose to depend on it.

## Claim Status Key

|Status|Meaning|
|---|---|
|`PROVABLE`|Source read, content confirmed, claim language calibrated to evidence.|
|`SUPPORTABLE`|Source exists and is directionally relevant; language must be softened to match evidence level.|
|`CONTESTED`|Source exists but has methodological limitations that must be named in book.|
|`THIN`|Source is advocacy, journalism, or lay; not peer-reviewed; must be labeled as such.|
|`MISSING`|No adequate source currently in `REFERENCES.md`; must be found before claim is made.|
|`WRONG SOURCE`|Source in list does not support this claim; must be corrected or removed.|
|`VERIFY`|Source has been added or identified, but the actual content has not yet been read closely enough to support prose.|

## Claim A: ABA shares historical roots with Lovaas-era conversion therapy

**Claim as currently implied in book:**

ABA and conversion therapy for gay or gender-nonconforming children share a historical lineage through Ivar Lovaas and behaviorist aversion/reinforcement methods.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 52: Gibson & Douglas, queer history of autism science / Lovaas scholarship
- Ref 126: Lovaas interview hosted at DocPlayer — content unconfirmed
- Ref 127: Wikipedia — Ole Ivar Lovaas; locator only
- Ref 128: LA Times obituary for Ivar Lovaas — journalism
- Ref 129: Crime and Justice PDF — content unconfirmed
- Ref 210: Rekers & Lovaas (1974), JABA DOI 10.1901/jaba.1974.7-173 — primary-source candidate

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE for narrow historical connection / CONTESTED for equivalence claim / VERIFY before detailed historical claims

**What these sources can support, if verified:**

Lovaas is connected to both early autism behavior-intervention history and behaviorist interventions aimed at suppressing gender-nonconforming behavior.

Ref 210 is the primary source for the Rekers & Lovaas 1974 gender-nonconforming behavior intervention claim.

Ref 52 is likely the stronger interpretive source for the queer-history frame.

Ref 128 can support public-history framing but remains journalism.

**What these sources do not prove:**

They do not prove that all modern ABA is conversion therapy.

They do not prove that modern ABA practitioners share Lovaas’s intentions.

They do not prove that every contemporary ABA program uses aversives.

They do not prove that harm mechanisms are identical between Autistic children and LGBTQ+ conversion-therapy survivors.

The book may argue continuity of behavioral logic, but it must not collapse categories without jurisdiction- and practice-specific proof.

**Book treatment:**

Safer:

> “Some ABA history directly overlaps with conversion-therapy history. That does not prove every present-day ABA practice is identical to conversion therapy, but it means normalization goals deserve careful scrutiny.”

Also safe, with attribution:

> “Some critics and legal scholars argue that behavior-modification programs suppressing autistic identity expression belong in the same conversion-practice family.”

Avoid:

> “ABA is conversion therapy.”

> “ABA is conversion therapy everywhere.”

**Action required:**

- Read Ref 210 before making claims about the 1974 paper’s contents.
- Read Ref 52 before using the queer-history interpretation.
- Read Ref 126 before using any Lovaas wording.
- Verify Ref 129 or remove it from ABA historical claims.
- If using “same behavioral framework,” define the framework carefully and avoid overclaiming sameness of intent or effect.

## Claim B: ABA or ABA-style intervention is legally classified with conversion practices in some jurisdictions or legal scholarship

**Claim as currently implied in book:**

Some legal frameworks or legal scholars classify ABA-style suppression of autistic identity expression alongside conversion therapy or conversion practices.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 37: Born Perfect conversion-therapy ban tracker — advocacy tracker
- Ref 38: Victorian Change or Suppression Practices legislation — primary legal source
- Ref 39: Equality Caucus international ban list — advocacy / policy tracker
- Ref 40: Human Rights Campaign reparative therapy resource — advocacy resource
- Ref 140: Cambridge Canadian Journal of Law and Society article on autistic and trans children in Ontario — peer-reviewed legal scholarship
- `References/CONVERSION-THERAPY-LEGAL-EVIDENCE-MAP.md`

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE with jurisdiction precision / THIN if based only on advocacy trackers / VERIFY before legal claims

**What these sources may support:**

Ref 38 can support claims about the exact language of Victorian law, if read directly.

Ref 140 can support claims about a legal-scholarship argument connecting clinical/legal personhood, autistic children, and trans children, if read directly.

Refs 37, 39, and 40 help locate policy context but should not carry statutory interpretation alone.

The dedicated legal map should carry the legal-only analysis so this file does not overstate jurisdictional claims.

**What these sources do not prove:**

They do not prove that ABA is universally illegal.

They do not prove that any US federal law classifies ABA as conversion therapy.

They do not prove that individual ABA practitioners have been prosecuted under conversion-therapy bans.

They do not prove that all behavior intervention is conversion practice.

The legally relevant questions are jurisdiction-specific and may involve suppression of identity expression, coercion, disability status, and statutory definitions.

**Book treatment:**

Safer:

> “In some legal and policy contexts, behavior-change practices aimed at suppressing identity expression are being analyzed alongside conversion practices. The exact legal status varies by jurisdiction.”

Also safe:

> “Some legal scholars and advocates have argued that behavioral suppression of identity expression belongs in the same ethical conversation as conversion practices.”

Avoid:

> “ABA is conversion therapy and is banned.”

**Action required:**

- Read Ref 38 for exact current statutory language.
- Read Ref 140 for the actual argument, jurisdiction, and scope.
- Verify whether any US state law explicitly names autism, ABA, disability, or behavior intervention before making US-specific claims.
- Use `References/CONVERSION-THERAPY-LEGAL-EVIDENCE-MAP.md` for legal-only claims.

## Claim C: ABA exposure is associated with PTSD or trauma symptoms in Autistic people

**Claim as currently implied in book:**

ABA exposure is associated with PTSD symptoms or trauma-like harm among Autistic people.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 55: Embrace Autism PTSD article — advocacy / educational source, not peer-reviewed
- Ref 137: Emerald / Advances in Autism DOI 10.1108/AIA-08-2017-0016 — appears to be Kupferstein (2018); verify before citing
- Refs 142–146: Autistic advocacy and lived-experience sources on ABA harm
- Ref 147: PMC7368197 — WRONG SOURCE for ABA/PTSD

**Status:** CONTESTED for peer-reviewed association if Ref 137 is confirmed / THIN for advocacy testimony / WRONG SOURCE for Ref 147

**Correction to prior map:**

Kupferstein (2018) is already present in `References/REFERENCES.md` as Ref 137.

The prior action item “Add Kupferstein” is resolved.

Ref 147 remains a wrong source for this claim.

**What these sources support, if read and characterized accurately:**

If Ref 137 is confirmed as Kupferstein (2018), it supports the limited claim that one peer-reviewed retrospective self-report study reported an association between ABA exposure and elevated PTSD symptom scores among Autistic respondents.

Refs 142–146 support the existence of consistent Autistic survivor testimony and community critique.

Survivor testimony is evidence of reported experience and should be treated seriously, clearly labeled, and not dismissed because it is not a randomized trial.

**Limitations that must travel with the claim:**

- Self-selected sample.
- Retrospective self-report.
- Confounding variables not fully controlled.
- Single study.
- Replication status uncertain.

**What these sources do not prove:**

They do not prove ABA causes PTSD.

A single retrospective, self-selected, self-report study cannot establish causality, prevalence, or generalizability.

Advocacy sources do not prove clinical mechanism.

Testimony does not prove that every ABA program harms every child, nor that all reported trauma arises from the same intervention components.

**Book treatment:**

Safer:

> “At least one peer-reviewed study has reported an association between ABA exposure and elevated PTSD symptoms, but the study is methodologically contested and should not be treated as causal proof. Autistic survivor testimony is also extensive and must be named as lived-experience evidence.”

Avoid:

> “Research proves ABA causes PTSD.”

**Action required:**

- Read Ref 137 and confirm title, sample, measures, recruitment, and limitations.
- Do not attach Ref 147 to ABA/PTSD claims.
- Search for replications, systematic reviews, critiques, and responses before strengthening the claim.

## Claim D: ABA often prioritizes compliance, indistinguishability, or passing over Autistic wellbeing

**Claim as currently implied in book:**

ABA and related autism outcome measures often reward appearing less Autistic rather than improving self-determined wellbeing.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 135: Fortune reporting on ABA industry — journalism
- Ref 136: Forbes reporting on ABA business practices — journalism
- Ref 138: Frontiers in Psychiatry article on Autistic client autonomy — peer-reviewed perspective
- Ref 141: Springer article — content unconfirmed
- Refs 142–146: advocacy / lived-experience sources
- Ref 209: Ne'eman, AMA Journal of Ethics, outcome measures should not promote passing — peer-reviewed ethics article

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE with source labels / PROVABLE for narrow outcome-measure ethics critique after Ref 209 verified

**What these sources support:**

Ref 209 directly supports the ethical critique that when disability is defined behaviorally, outcome measures can wrongly promote “passing” or typical appearance over person-centered goals.

Ref 138 supports a broader autonomy critique in mental health treatment involving Autistic clients, though it is a perspective article rather than an empirical trial.

Refs 135–136 document industry and financial-incentive concerns as journalism.

Refs 142–146 document Autistic advocacy and lived-experience objections.

**What these sources do not prove:**

They do not prove every ABA program prioritizes compliance over wellbeing.

They do not prove that compliance-focused outcomes are unique to ABA.

They do not prove the size of the effect across the whole ABA industry.

They do not prove that reducing intrinsically dangerous or distressing behavior is unethical.

The critique applies most strongly when harmless Autistic traits are targeted for suppression because they are stigmatized.

**Book treatment:**

Safer:

> “A serious ethical critique of ABA and autism outcome measurement is that some interventions define success as appearing less Autistic. That can conflict with self-determination when the targeted trait is harmless and only socially stigmatized.”

Also safe:

> “Autistic critics have argued that some intervention goals reward appearing less Autistic rather than becoming safer, freer, or better supported. Ari Ne'eman argues in the AMA Journal of Ethics that outcome measures should not promote passing when disability is defined by behavior.”

Avoid:

> “ABA is only about compliance.”

**Action required:**

- Read and confirm Ref 141 before using it.
- Identify additional empirical work on autism intervention outcome measures, especially work distinguishing quality of life, autonomy, communication, safety, and compliance proxies.
- When discussing lived experience, label it as lived experience rather than clinical-trial evidence.

## Claim E: ABA front-line delivery can involve low formal training relative to intervention intensity

**Claim as currently implied in book:**

Registered Behavior Technicians can deliver intensive behavior-analytic services to Autistic children after relatively limited training, under supervision.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 148: Discovery ABA salary source — industry source
- Ref 149: NCES education statistics — federal education data
- Ref 150: Psych Central professional statistics — health journalism
- Ref 151: The Tree Top ABA demographics — industry-adjacent
- Ref 152: Songbird Care RBT certification — industry source
- Ref 153: Elemy degree requirements — industry source
- Ref 154: EdSource teacher credential comparison — education journalism
- Ref 213: BACB Registered Behavior Technician page — credentialing authority

**Status:** PROVABLE for credential structure once BACB source/handbook is read / THIN for harm inference

**What these sources can support:**

Ref 213 is the appropriate primary source for the RBT credential structure and supervision relationship.

It can support the claim that RBT is a paraprofessional certification and that RBTs practice under supervision, once exact current requirements are read and captured.

Teacher comparisons may be supportable with NCES / California credentialing sources, but must be jurisdiction-specific.

Industry and journalism sources can help with labor-market context, but the credential claim should use BACB directly.

**What these sources do not prove:**

They do not prove that lower formal training causes harm.

They do not prove that all RBTs are unprepared.

They do not prove that supervision is categorically inadequate.

They do not prove that teacher credential comparisons are perfectly equivalent.

Teacher comparisons may be useful as reader-facing context, but not as a controlled professional-risk analysis.

**Book treatment:**

Safer:

> “The front-line role is paraprofessional and supervised. Given the intensity and developmental stakes of the work, the training/supervision model deserves scrutiny.”

Also safe:

> “The front-line RBT credential has a much lower training threshold than many readers may expect for someone working intensively with disabled children. That fact raises a governance question: what safeguards, supervision, and consent structures are enough?”

Avoid:

> “Barely trained workers are doing all this damage.”

**Action required:**

- Read the current BACB RBT page and, if available, RBT Handbook before claiming exact eligibility, training, competency assessment, exam, supervision, and transition requirements.
- Prefer BACB over ABA provider marketing pages.
- Verify any teacher-comparison number by jurisdiction and source.

## Claim F: ABA’s origins include aversive conditioning, including electric shock, and aversive devices persisted into recent regulatory history

**Claim as currently implied in book:**

Early ABA used aversive interventions including electric shock, and electrical stimulation devices used as aversives remained a live regulatory issue into the 2020s.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 126: Lovaas interview — primary-source candidate, content unconfirmed
- Ref 128: LA Times obituary — journalism
- Ref 211: Federal Register 2020 FDA final rule banning electrical stimulation devices for self-injurious or aggressive behavior
- Ref 212: Reuters 2024 reporting on FDA proposal to ban certain shock devices again

**Status:** PROVABLE for FDA regulatory action / SUPPORTABLE for Lovaas-era history after source verification / VERIFY legal timeline before current-status claim

**What these sources can support:**

Ref 126 may support direct claims about Lovaas’s own words, if read and verified carefully.

Ref 128 can support a journalistic obituary-level summary of Lovaas’s use of aversives.

Ref 211 can support that FDA issued a 2020 final rule banning certain electrical stimulation devices for self-injurious or aggressive behavior, and should be used for the exact regulatory scope.

Ref 212 can support later regulatory/reporting context, but it is journalism and does not itself settle current legal status.

**What these sources do not prove:**

They do not prove that current mainstream ABA uses electric shock.

They do not prove that every aversive device use is identical to every ABA program.

They do not prove that the FDA rule was a ban on ABA as a whole.

They do not prove that the 2020 FDA ban remained continuously in force without litigation or legal change.

The current claim must distinguish early ABA history, the Judge Rotenberg Center / device context, and contemporary mainstream ABA practice.

**Book treatment:**

Safer:

> “ABA’s early history includes aversive conditioning, including electric shock in Lovaas-era work. Separately, US regulators treated electrical stimulation devices for self-injurious or aggressive behavior as dangerous enough to ban in 2020. That is not the same as saying all modern ABA uses shock.”

Also safe:

> “Early ABA history included aversive conditioning, including electric shock. Later regulatory fights over electrical stimulation devices show that the aversive-history question is not merely ancient history. The exact legal timeline should be stated carefully.”

Avoid:

> “ABA still uses electric shock.”

**Action required:**

- Read Ref 126 before quoting Lovaas.
- Read Ref 211 and verify the exact regulatory scope.
- Confirm procedural status of the FDA ban and later court/regulatory developments before publishing a final chronology.
- Find court / FDA follow-up sources for legal status after 2020.

## What This Map Is Not

This map does not settle the question of whether ABA harms Autistic people.

The evidence that it may — survivor testimony, the Kupferstein study, conversion-therapy overlap arguments, credential comparisons, industry incentive documentation, and aversive-history records — is real and serious.

The evidence is also incomplete, contested, and in some places thin.

The book’s job is to hold both of those things at once:

to speak with teeth about what is documented,

and to speak with precision about what is not yet proved.

The reader deserves both.
