## Purpose

This file maps legal-classification claims about conversion therapy, conversion practices, ABA-adjacent behavior suppression, and jurisdiction-specific legal language. These claims matter because legal claims can be powerful and dangerous: the book may name real overlap between coercive normalization practices, but must not imply universal bans, US federal classification, or prosecution history without primary legal proof. For ABA-specific overlap, cross-reference `References/ABA-EVIDENCE-MAP.md` rather than duplicating its full ABA analysis.

## Claim A: Conversion therapy is legally restricted or banned in multiple jurisdictions

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Conversion therapy has been legally banned or restricted in multiple states, countries, and jurisdictions.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 37: Born Perfect — conversion therapy bans by state
- Ref 38: Victoria, Australia — Change or Suppression Practices Prohibition Bill 2020 / legal source
- Ref 39: Equality Caucus — countries that banned conversion therapy
- Ref 40: Human Rights Campaign — “The Lies and Dangers of Reparative Therapy”

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE as policy-context claim / PROVABLE only jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction from primary legal texts

**What these sources prove:**
Refs 37 and 39 support that advocacy and policy organizations track conversion-therapy bans across jurisdictions. Ref 38 is the strongest source type because it is a primary legal source for one jurisdiction. Ref 40 supports advocacy/educational framing about reparative therapy and its harms, but it is not itself a statute or court decision.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Advocacy trackers do not prove exact current legal status in every jurisdiction without checking primary legal texts. They do not prove enforcement history, case outcomes, constitutional status, scope of covered professionals, age ranges, religious exemptions, or whether a ban covers disability-related behavior intervention. They also do not prove that the law is identical across countries or states.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “Conversion therapy is banned or restricted in a growing number of jurisdictions, though definitions and scope vary.” Use primary legal text for any specific jurisdiction. Do not write a blanket claim such as “conversion therapy is illegal everywhere” or “every conversion therapy ban covers ABA.”

**Action required:**
Verify ref 37 and ref 39 against official statutory or government sources before final publication. Use ref 38 as the model: legal claims should be anchored in primary law, not only policy trackers.

## Claim B: Some legal frameworks define conversion practices broadly enough to raise questions about suppressing identity expression beyond sexuality alone

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Some conversion-practice laws or legal analyses may apply to coercive practices that suppress identity expression, including disability/autistic expression in some contexts.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 38: Victorian legal source
- Ref 140: Cambridge Canadian Journal of Law and Society article on autistic and trans children in Ontario — cross-referenced in `References/ABA-EVIDENCE-MAP.md`, not duplicated here

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE with precise legal language / MISSING exact final statutory analysis in this file

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 38 can prove what the Victorian legal framework says once the current enacted text and definitions are read. Ref 140, as mapped in `References/ABA-EVIDENCE-MAP.md`, appears to be the relevant legal-scholarship source for connecting autistic and trans children, clinical personhood, and conversion-practice reasoning in Ontario.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not prove that all ABA is legally conversion therapy. They do not prove that all disability-related behavior intervention is covered by conversion-therapy law. They do not prove that US law has adopted this classification. They do not prove that identity-expression suppression and safety-focused intervention are legally identical.

**Book treatment:**
Write with legal humility: “Some legal scholars and some broad conversion-practice frameworks raise the question of whether coercive suppression of autistic expression belongs in the same legal and ethical family as other conversion practices.” Use “raise the question” or “argue,” unless and until a statute or ruling explicitly says more.

**Action required:**
Read ref 38 for exact definitions: protected attributes, covered conduct, covered providers, exemptions, and enforcement. Read ref 140 and summarize its actual legal thesis, not just the title.

## Claim C: HRC and Born Perfect support harm and policy framing, but they do not substitute for primary law or clinical research

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
HRC and Born Perfect prove that conversion therapy is dangerous and banned, and can be used to support legal classification claims.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 37: Born Perfect ban tracker
- Ref 40: Human Rights Campaign reparative therapy resource

**Status:** THIN for legal proof / SUPPORTABLE for advocacy-policy context

**What these sources prove:**
Refs 37 and 40 document advocacy-community framing, policy tracking, and public education around conversion therapy and reparative therapy. They are useful for understanding movement language and for finding primary sources.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not independently prove clinical causality, statutory scope, constitutional validity, enforcement outcomes, or jurisdiction-specific definitions. They should not be quoted as if they are courts, legislatures, systematic reviews, or professional association clinical guidelines.

**Book treatment:**
Use advocacy sources openly: “Advocacy organizations such as Born Perfect and HRC track bans and summarize the harms claimed by survivors and professional opponents of conversion therapy.” Then separately cite primary law or peer-reviewed research for legal or clinical proof.

**Action required:**
For any state/country claim drawn from ref 37 or ref 39, add the official law. For harm claims drawn from ref 40, add professional association statements and peer-reviewed or systematic-review sources where possible.

## Claim D: ABA legal-overlap claims belong in the ABA evidence map and should be cross-referenced, not duplicated

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
The conversion-therapy legal classification group overlaps with ABA claims.

**Sources in current list:**

- Refs 37, 38, 39, 40
- Cross-reference: `References/ABA-EVIDENCE-MAP.md`, especially Claim B

**Status:** CROSS-REFERENCE REQUIRED

**What these sources prove:**
The same legal/policy sources are relevant to ABA only where the book claims that suppressing autistic identity expression resembles, overlaps with, or may fall within conversion-practice reasoning. `References/ABA-EVIDENCE-MAP.md` already maps the ABA-specific risks: overstatement, jurisdiction precision, and the difference between legal analogy and universal classification.

**What these sources do not prove:**
The legal conversion-therapy sources do not prove the ABA history claims, PTSD claims, training claims, aversive-shock claims, or outcome-measure claims. Those remain separate ABA evidence questions. They also do not prove that all behavior intervention is illegal or conversion therapy.

**Book treatment:**
In this file, keep the legal structure clean. In the ABA file, discuss ABA-specific evidence. Use cross-reference language rather than copy/paste duplication.

**Action required:**
When drafting prose, decide whether the claim is legal, clinical, historical, or ethical. Then cite the right evidence map. Do not make one source do all four jobs.
