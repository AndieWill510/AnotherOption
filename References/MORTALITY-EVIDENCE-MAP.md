## Purpose

This file maps lifespan, premature mortality, suicide, epilepsy/SUDEP, and lifespan-comparison claims to the sources currently listed in `Chapters/REFERENCES.md`. These claims matter because mortality language is emotionally and ethically high-stakes: the book may name real danger, but must not turn population-risk evidence into individual doom, flatten autistic heterogeneity, or use wrong-slot references as proof.

|Status        |Meaning                                                                                       |

## Claim A: Autistic people experience elevated premature mortality at the population level

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Autistic people, as a population, die earlier than non-autistic comparison groups.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 1: Hirvikoski et al., *The British Journal of Psychiatry*, “Premature mortality in autism spectrum disorder”
- Ref 46: South et al., *JAMA Network Open*, invited commentary on suicide among people with autism
- Ref 47: JAMA article ID 2513561 — appears to be income/life expectancy, not autism mortality

**Status:** PROVABLE from ref 1 for Swedish register cohort / WRONG SOURCE for ref 47

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 1 is a population-based Swedish register cohort. It compared 27,122 people diagnosed with ASD between 1987 and 2009 with 2,672,185 matched controls. During the observed period, 706 autistic people died (2.60%) compared with 24,358 controls (0.91%), with an overall odds ratio of 2.56. It supports a calibrated population-level claim that premature mortality was markedly elevated in this cohort and that elevated mortality occurred across many cause categories.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Ref 1 does not prove a universal fixed autistic life expectancy. It does not prove that every autistic person will die early, that autism itself directly causes death, or that the same risk ratios apply unchanged across countries, cohorts, service systems, diagnostic eras, race/ethnicity groups, gender-diverse groups, or undiagnosed autistic adults. It is registry-based and shaped by who was diagnosed, how diagnosis was recorded, and Swedish health-system context.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “Large register studies have found elevated premature mortality among diagnosed autistic people as a population.” Do not write: “Autistic people die at age X” unless a source specifically supports that number and its population, method, and uncertainty are named.

**Action required:**
Keep ref 1 as the main mortality anchor. Replace or reassign ref 47. Add a current systematic review/meta-analysis of autism mortality before making broad international claims.

## Claim B: Suicide risk is elevated for autistic people, especially in some subgroups

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Autistic people face elevated suicide attempt and suicide death risk, with particular concern for women/girls, people diagnosed later, people without intellectual disability, and people with co-occurring psychiatric conditions.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 1: Hirvikoski et al., mortality cohort
- Ref 46: South et al., invited commentary on Kõlves et al. Danish population study

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE from current list / MISSING the primary Danish suicide study

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 1 supports that suicide was a notable cause-specific mortality concern in the Swedish cohort and that suicide risk patterns differed by intellectual ability. Ref 46 is not the primary study; it is an invited commentary. It reports that the related Danish population-based study used more than 6.5 million people over 10 years and found adjusted incidence rate ratios more than 3 times higher for autistic individuals for suicide attempts and deaths, with especially high reported attempt ratios for autistic girls and women and for autistic people with additional psychiatric conditions.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Ref 46 does not itself provide primary data. It cannot carry the claim alone. The current list does not yet prove exact suicide prevalence, causal pathways, or which interventions reduce risk. It also does not prove that masking, diagnostic overshadowing, gender, trauma, isolation, or psychiatric comorbidity each independently cause suicidality; those claims need separate sources.

**Book treatment:**
Use urgency without fatalism: “Population studies and expert commentary identify suicide as a serious and elevated risk for autistic people, especially where depression, anxiety, late diagnosis, isolation, or lack of support are present.” Do not frame autism itself as a death sentence.

**Action required:**
Add the primary study discussed by ref 46: Kõlves et al., “Assessment of Suicidal Behaviors Among Individuals With Autism Spectrum Disorder in Denmark,” *JAMA Network Open* 2021, DOI 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2020.33565. Add a current suicidality systematic review/meta-analysis if this claim will recur in the book.

## Claim C: Epilepsy and SUDEP are relevant mortality risks in autistic populations

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Epilepsy and seizure-related death, including SUDEP, are important safety concerns for autistic people because epilepsy is more common in autistic populations.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 1: Hirvikoski et al., cause-specific mortality cohort
- Ref 100: Epilepsy Foundation page on SUDEP and seizure alerts

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE for safety framing / MISSING autism-specific epilepsy prevalence source

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 1 supports elevated mortality in nervous-system disease categories among autistic people in the Swedish cohort and can support a broad claim that neurological conditions matter in autism mortality. Ref 100 is a practical safety source about SUDEP and seizure alerts; it can support caregiver-facing safety awareness about seizures and early death risk in epilepsy.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Ref 100 does not prove autism-specific epilepsy prevalence or autism-specific SUDEP rates. It does not prove that seizure-alert devices prevent death in autistic people. Ref 1 does not by itself provide a full clinical guidance pathway for epilepsy management. The book should not imply that all autistic people are at seizure-related mortality risk.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “For autistic people who also have epilepsy, seizure safety and SUDEP awareness matter.” Avoid making SUDEP sound like a general autistic risk independent of epilepsy.

**Action required:**
Add an autism-specific epilepsy prevalence or comorbidity review if making autism-plus-epilepsy claims. Verify current Epilepsy Foundation guidance before practical safety recommendations.

## Claim D: Down syndrome lifespan comparison can illustrate that social/medical support changes life expectancy

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Down syndrome lifespan improvements show that population life expectancy is not destiny; medical care, inclusion, and social support can change outcomes.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 122: Journal of Pediatrics article URL with article identifier S0022-3476(13)00726-9 — content unconfirmed
- Ref 121: CDC Down syndrome data — background, not part of requested T1 set
- Refs 124–125: Down syndrome health/facts pages — background, not part of requested T1 set

**Status:** T2 / content unconfirmed for ref 122; SUPPORTABLE only after verification

**What these sources prove:**
At this drafting stage, ref 122 proves only that a Journal of Pediatrics URL exists in the reference list. Its exact title, design, sample, and claim relationship must be verified before use. If confirmed as a Down syndrome longevity source, it may support a comparison claim about lifespan change over time, but that is not yet established in this evidence map.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Ref 122 does not currently prove any claim until read. Even if it is a Down syndrome longevity article, it would not prove that autism mortality will follow the same trajectory, that the mechanisms are the same, or that support alone explains all lifespan change. Down syndrome comparison must remain analogy, not autism proof.

**Book treatment:**
Use only after verification and label as analogy: “Other disability communities show that lifespan statistics can change when care, inclusion, and medical support change.” Do not use Down syndrome evidence as direct proof of autistic mortality mechanisms.

**Action required:**
Identify ref 122 title, DOI, study design, sample, and conclusion. If it cannot be verified, replace it with a known primary source on Down syndrome life expectancy trends.
