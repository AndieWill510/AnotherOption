## Purpose

This file maps claims about autistic masking/camouflaging and harm. These claims matter because masking is central to the book’s account of survival, exhaustion, delayed recognition, and self-loss; the evidence must honor survivor testimony while distinguishing qualitative evidence, cross-sectional association, suicidality research, and wrong-slot stigma sources.

## Claim A: Ref 188 does not support the masking/harm claim currently assigned to it

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Ref 188 is a peer-reviewed masking study that proves masking harms autistic people.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 188: BMC Medicine article, DOI 10.1186/s12916-019-1271-3

**Status:** WRONG SOURCE

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 188 is a real BMC Medicine source, but the URL resolves to the “Health Stigma and Discrimination Framework,” not to an autism masking/camouflaging study. It may be useful elsewhere for structural stigma or discrimination theory, but not as the primary proof for autistic masking harm.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Ref 188 does not prove autistic masking exists, does not provide a masking sample, does not measure autistic camouflaging, does not establish association with exhaustion, depression, anxiety, suicidality, identity confusion, delayed diagnosis, or burnout, and should not be cited as a masking study.

**Book treatment:**
Do not cite ref 188 for masking. If stigma framework language is useful elsewhere, move it to a stigma evidence map. For masking, use actual camouflaging literature.

**Action required:**
Correct the triage register entry for ref 188. Add actual masking/camouflaging studies to `Chapters/REFERENCES.md` before using the masking/harm claim.

## Claim B: Autistic adults report masking/camouflaging as effortful and associated with exhaustion, identity threat, and mental-health strain

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Masking is not just a harmless social skill; autistic people report it as exhausting, identity-distorting, and psychologically costly.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 188: WRONG SOURCE for this claim
- Ref 187: Embrace Autism CAT-Q scores — advocacy/educational source, adjacent but not requested T1 proof
- Ref 161: Devon Price, *Unmasking Autism* — book / advocacy-adjacent, not peer-reviewed

**Status:** MISSING from current T1 list / SUPPORTABLE after adding primary studies

**What these sources prove:**
The current T1 source assigned to the claim proves nothing about autistic masking. However, specific verified sources exist outside the current references: Hull et al. (2017), “Putting on My Best Normal,” is a qualitative open-access study in the *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* with 92 diagnosed autistic adults. It used thematic analysis and reported motivations including fitting in and connection, techniques including masking and compensation, and consequences including exhaustion, challenging stereotypes, and threats to self-perception.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Qualitative evidence does not prove population prevalence, causality, or the size of mental-health effects. It does not prove every autistic person masks, that every masking behavior is harmful in every context, or that all social adaptation is inherently abusive. It also does not prove that masking is the sole cause of burnout, suicidality, depression, anxiety, or delayed diagnosis.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “Autistic adults in qualitative research describe masking as a survival strategy that can bring short-term safety or acceptance while also producing exhaustion, self-alienation, and mental-health costs.” Keep “can” and “describe” unless stronger quantitative evidence is added.

**Action required:**
Add Hull et al. (2017), DOI 10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5, to `Chapters/REFERENCES.md` and map it as primary masking evidence. Capture study design, n=92, recruitment, diagnostic verification limits, and thematic-analysis method.

## Claim C: Masking/camouflaging is associated with suicidality or suicide-relevant mechanisms in some studies

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Masking increases suicide risk for autistic people or people with high autistic traits.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 188: WRONG SOURCE for this claim
- Ref 46: Suicide commentary, not a masking study

**Status:** MISSING from current references / CONTESTED once added

**What these sources prove:**
The current source list does not prove a masking-suicidality claim. A verified candidate source exists outside the list: Cassidy et al. (2020), “Is Camouflaging Autistic Traits Associated with Suicidal Thoughts and Behaviours?,” *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*. It was a cross-sectional online survey of 160 UK undergraduate psychology students, 86.9% female and aged 18–23, using self-report measures of thwarted belongingness, perceived burdensomeness, autistic traits, depression, anxiety, camouflaging, and lifetime suicidality. It found an association between camouflaging autistic traits and increased risk of thwarted belongingness and lifetime suicidality.

**What these sources do not prove:**
That study does not prove causality. It is cross-sectional, self-report, mostly female, undergraduate, and not necessarily a clinically diagnosed autistic sample. It does not prove that masking causes suicide, nor that the same association size applies to autistic children, older autistic adults, nonspeaking autistic people, autistic people with intellectual disability, or non-UK populations.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “Some cross-sectional studies link camouflaging with suicide-relevant distress, including thwarted belongingness and lifetime suicidality. These findings are serious but associative, not causal.” Do not write: “Masking causes suicide.”

**Action required:**
Add Cassidy et al. (2020), DOI 10.1007/s10803-019-04323-3, and any later autistic-sample studies or systematic reviews. Distinguish autistic-diagnosed samples from broader-autistic-trait samples.

## Claim D: Survivor testimony about masking is real evidence, but it must be labeled correctly

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Autistic people consistently describe masking as survival under social threat, not merely voluntary etiquette.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 161: Devon Price, *Unmasking Autism*
- Ref 187: Embrace Autism CAT-Q scores
- Potentially refs 142–146 if used for ABA/masking overlap testimony

**Status:** THIN for research proof / SUPPORTABLE as lived-experience evidence

**What these sources prove:**
These sources can document the existence of autistic community language, self-report, survivor analysis, and tools used by autistic people to understand masking. They are legitimate evidence of reported experience and community meaning. They are valuable for voice, framing, and phenomenology.

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not establish clinical causality, prevalence, diagnostic validity, or intervention outcomes. They do not substitute for peer-reviewed studies when the book makes claims about mental-health risk. They also do not prove that every autistic person wants to unmask in every context; safety may require strategic masking.

**Book treatment:**
Use testimony as testimony, not as lab evidence. A strong book sentence can say: “Autistic people have been naming the cost of masking for years; peer-reviewed research is beginning to measure what survivors already described.”

**Action required:**
Separate the “lived-experience evidence” paragraph from the “peer-reviewed evidence” paragraph in manuscript notes so neither is flattened or dismissed.
