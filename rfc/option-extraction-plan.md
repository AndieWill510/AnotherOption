# Option Extraction Plan

Author: Kevin "Andie" Williams  
Status: Draft v0.1  
Project: You Have Another Option  
Repository: AnotherOption  
Date: 2026-05-30

---

## 1. Purpose

This document defines how the imported manuscript chunks become short reader-facing Options.

The source files in `Chapters/` are protected source bins.

They are not final chapters.

They preserve source material, sequence, and provenance while the book is reshaped into a survivable reader interface.

---

## 2. Controlling Rule

> One private sitting. One survivable idea. One concrete next move.

Each Option should be readable by a person who may be overwhelmed, hiding, dysregulated, ashamed, exhausted, or trying to get through the next hour.

The Option should not require the reader to carry an entire theory system before receiving relief.

---

## 3. Target Unit

Each Option should usually be:

- 700–1,500 words;
- 5–8 minutes of reading;
- one primary idea;
- one practical next move;
- one moment of mercy, recognition, or reframe.

Hard ceiling:

- approximately 2,000 words unless there is a clear reader-safety reason to keep it intact.

If a draft Option contains more than one major idea, split it.

If an Option becomes a lecture, cut or split it.

If an Option asks too much of a dysregulated reader, repair it.

---

## 4. Option Shape

Default internal structure:

1. **Doorway** — story, image, or direct recognition.
2. **Pressure** — the harm, stressor, shame pattern, or risk.
3. **Reframe** — the idea that changes the reader’s relationship to the pressure.
4. **Move** — one concrete action, question, practice, boundary, or support step.
5. **Mercy** — closing line that reduces shame and preserves hope.

The structure may be invisible in final prose.

It should still guide extraction.

---

## 5. Naming Convention

Use `Option` as the small reader-facing unit.

Rationale:

- the title is *You Have Another Option*;
- the reader should repeatedly experience choice returning;
- the word is practical, gentle, non-clinical, and non-heroic;
- it gives the book a repeatable survival interface.

Format:

```text
Option <reader-facing chapter>.<sequence> — <Title>
```

Examples:

```text
Option 1.1 — The Saxophone
Option 1.3 — You Were Never Going to Make Those Notes
Option 4.2 — You Are Not Stimming Enough
Option 9.2 — Learn to Swim Before You Need the Water
Option 9.5 — Call 988 Before You Negotiate
```

---

## 6. Extraction Workflow

For each source bin:

1. Identify the major reader-useful ideas.
2. Mark any content that belongs in back matter, evidence notes, or reference store.
3. Extract candidate Options.
4. Give each Option a provisional title.
5. Map the Option to one proposed reader-facing chapter.
6. Flag safety-sensitive material.
7. Flag citation-sensitive or overclaim-risk material.
8. Leave fine-grained prose editing for a later pass.

Do not force every paragraph into an Option.

Some material should become:

- footnotes;
- endnotes;
- reference store;
- author notes;
- cut material;
- future companion material;
- workbook exercises;
- web appendix;
- prompts for C challenge.

## 8. Safety Flags

Mark an Option with a safety flag when it includes:

- suicide or self-harm discussion;
- abuse or domestic violence;
- ABA trauma;
- sexual content;
- psychosis, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, or substance use;
- homelessness;
- medical advice-adjacent content;
- mortality/lifespan claims;
- crisis instructions;
- claims about children.

Safety-flagged Options require review before reader-facing publication.

---

## 9. Citation / Evidence Flags

Mark an Option with an evidence flag when it includes:

- lifespan estimates;
- suicide-risk claims;
- drowning / choking / epilepsy statistics;
- claims about ABA harms;
- clinical modality comparisons;
- claims about hormones, cortisol, oxytocin, dopamine, endorphins, trauma physiology, or immune effects;
- claims about gender, sexuality, race, disability, or diagnosis prevalence;
- claims about service dogs, treatment efficacy, or medication.

Evidence flags do not mean the claim is wrong.

They mean the claim needs receipts, humility, or narrowing.

---

## 10. Cut / Hold / Move Rules

Do not cut solely because a passage is intense.

Cut, hold, or move when:

- the passage serves the author more than the reader;
- the passage is true but too heavy for the main path;
- the passage needs research support not yet available;
- the passage creates reader shame;
- the passage repeats an idea already carried better elsewhere;
- the passage belongs in back matter, reference notes, or a companion essay.

Preserve cut material in a holding area if possible.

Do not erase provenance.

---

## 11. Pilot Scope

Pilot first on Part I — The Doorway.

Reason:

If the doorway is right, the book lives.

Pilot source:

- saxophone prelude;
- preface mortality material;
- `010` Introduction;
- relevant material from `000` and README culture.

Pilot output:

```text
Part I — The Doorway

Option 1.1 — The Saxophone
Option 1.2 — The Broken Tool
Option 1.3 — You Were Never Going to Make Those Notes
Option 1.4 — The Lifespan Shock
Option 1.5 — This Book Is Another Option
```

Pilot success criteria:

- reader feels seen by Option 1.1;
- mortality urgency enters without crushing the reader;
- book promise is clear by the end of Part I;
- Options are short enough to read in one private pause;
- no option relies on false certainty;
- no option creates shame for still struggling.

---

## 12. C Review Timing

C should review before large-scale extraction, but after the architecture and extraction plan exist.

C should not fine-edit voice yet.

C should challenge:

- the 7-part / 12-chapter / Options scaffold;
- the safety model;
- the placement of sensitive material;
- the risk of overclaiming;
- the risk of reader overwhelm;
- the doorway sequence;
- the treatment of Part VI;
- the use of `Option` as the reader-facing unit.
